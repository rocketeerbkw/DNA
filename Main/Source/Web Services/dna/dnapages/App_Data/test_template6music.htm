Has the Mercurys lost it?

So, the Mercury nominations are finally out. The hotly anticipated list of twelve acts was revealed on Tuesday morning - and right on cue, William Hill unleashed their favourite odds on Amy Winehouse and Arctic Monkeys. But then, everyone guessed that - right? As was the predicted Dizzee Rascal nomination. And Klaxons. And Jamie T…
In fact, did you, like us on The Music Week, pretty much predict the majority of the nominees? Possibly so…and that’s exactly the question we should be asking - has the Mercury Music Prize become too obvious?


Back in the day, the Mercurys always managed to conjure up a nominations list to spark up some surprises. Remember when The Spice Girls and Radiohead's ‘OK Computer’ went on the same list in 1997?! N'est pas!

The choice of winner was always a source of debate as well - Gomez winning over Pulp back in 1998 being the prime example. But, as some may argue, it was the Mercurys doing what it always should have done - championing the British underdog and supporting the lesser known acts needing the boost both in recognition and sales.

But what of the Mercurys of late? Should Amy Winehouse and Arctic Monkeys - as brilliant as their albums are - even be on the list? With the market of music award ceremonies being utterly saturated- shouldn’t The Mercury’s, more than ever, be championing more British leftfield acts? Or are we being way to harsh? Should we just be saluting the judges for picking twelve perfectly decent British albums - regardless whether they’re established artists or not?
